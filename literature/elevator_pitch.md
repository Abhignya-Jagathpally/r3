# Multiple Myeloma Single-Cell Genomics: Elevator Pitch (5 Minutes)

## For a Smart Non-Expert

### 1. One-Sentence Version of What This Field Has Proven

Single-cell genomics shows that multiple myeloma is not a simple disease of one cancerous clone, but a complex ecosystem where cancer cells and immune cells co-evolve—and patients with the most exhausted immune systems at diagnosis get sick the fastest.

---

### 2. One Honest Admission of What It Still Doesn't Know

We can describe the problem beautifully (cancer creates exhausted T cells), we can predict who will relapse using immune features (early relapsers have a specific immune signature), but we don't actually know whether fixing the exhaustion will prevent relapse—no one has tried.

---

### 3. Single Real-World Implication That Matters Most

**Implication**: Your myeloma prognosis might be determined less by the cancer's genetics and more by whether your immune system can fight back. This means new treatments might need to focus on waking up your T cells, not just killing cancer cells.

**Why it matters**: If your doctor tests your immune health at diagnosis (not just genetics), they can identify you as high-risk early and start aggressive immune-reactivation therapy before relapse happens—changing outcomes from months to years.

---

## No Jargon, No Hedging, No Academic Throat-Clearing

**What happens**: A blood test shows you have cancer. Doctors run genetic tests on the cancer (looking for "bad mutations") and decide on treatment. Most patients do well initially, then the cancer comes back.

**What single-cell genomics revealed**: Cancer cells come in many varieties within your body, and your bone marrow has immune cells supposed to kill them. In patients who relapse fast, those immune cells are exhausted—burnt out, unable to fight. This exhaustion happens *before* relapse (we see it at diagnosis), suggesting it causes the relapse.

**What we now do**: Doctors can now also test your immune cells at diagnosis (not just cancer genetics). If your immune cells look exhausted, you're flagged as super-high-risk. This wasn't possible five years ago.

**What we still don't know**: Can we un-exhaust your immune cells? We have drugs that might work (senolytics, checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T cells), but no one has formally tested whether using these drugs to restore immunity actually prevents relapse in myeloma. It seems obvious it should work, but science requires proof.

**Bottom line**: Single-cell genomics has rewritten the textbook—myeloma might not be primarily a cancer problem; it might be primarily an immune system problem. If that's true, future cures will come from restoring immunity, not from better cancer-killing drugs alone.
